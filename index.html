Over the past three decades, certain companies have endured as divestment targets for pro-Palestinian activists, including Boeing, Caterpillar, Lockheed Martin, and Raytheon.

The 2002, for example, cites Boeing and General Electric as makers of Apache helicopters, which the authors write “regularly used by the Israeli military in carrying out extra-judicial killings.” It also cites Caterpillar as the maker of D9 Bulldozers which have been used to “to raze entire neighborhoods” in the West Bank and Gaza.

The Eye obtained a draft version of the 2007 Iraq Divestment Proposal which preceded later more general arms divestment proposals. ACSRI rejected the Iraq divestment proposal.

ACSRI continued considering proposals that demanded divestment from arms in 2008 and then an updated proposal to divest from cluster munitions in 2009. ACSRI responded with subcommittees to provide more research on arms and cluster munitions.

ACSRI ultimately never made a recommendation to the board of trustees.

On February 2, 2016, Spectator reported that Columbia’s Students for Justice in Palestine and Columbia and Barnard’s Jewish Voice for Peace groups publicly united as Columbia University Apartheid Divest. This iteration of CUAD did not submit a proposal to ACSRI. The Eye reported in 2019 that CUAD had lost faith in ACSRI’s effectiveness. Instead, they called on Columbia to divest from eight corporations, supported by a faculty-led petition.

Spectator reported that the decision to target these eight corporations was symbolic as CUAD at the time did not know whether Columbia had holdings in them but that CUAD stated it was “highly likely” that Columbia has a stake in these corporations.

CUAD, in its new configuration, wrote a proposal to ACSRI, citing divestment targets that they could prove Columbia directly invests in—Airbnb—or were connected to given Columbia’s status as the sole beneficiary of a trust that provides Columbia grants and contributions.

CUAD highlights that Airbnb had listed 39 settlements in the occupied West Bank at the time of their proposal and Microsoft provides services to the Israeli Ministry of Defense and owns Azure, a cloud computing platform that contributes to “illicit surveillance” of Palestinians in the West Bank and Gaza.

The three proposals related to divestment from Israel and human rights violations more broadly list Ametek and Teledyne, emphasizing their perceived significance by activists as publicly-traded securities that the Columbia University Investment Management Company manages as their direct holdings.

Additional companies are included in the proposal asking Columbia to exercise its “rights as a shareholder.” This would encourage the university to use its shareholder stake as leverage to demand companies within its investment portfolio cease any activities that are “complicit in human rights violations and violations of U.S. export and foreign assistance laws which infringe upon the rights of Palestinians in Israel, Occupied Palestinian Territories, and the Palestinian Diaspora.” Columbia is connected to many of these companies as a beneficiary of these trusts.

Teledyne is an American defense contractor that has at least 124 of these contracts that supply weapons to Israel, according to their proposal. Campaign Against Arms Trade, a UK-based organisation working to end the international arms trade, lists the most recent contract between Teledyne and Israel from last May.

Ametek is a company that produces cooling components for F-16 and F-53 fighter jets. The proposals cites a 2009 airstrike when the United Nations documented how F-16 jets were employed by the Israeli Air Force to conduct airstrikes in Gaza during Operation Cast Lead. During that 22-day assault, Israel’s military killed at least 1400 Palestinians, 300 of which were children.
